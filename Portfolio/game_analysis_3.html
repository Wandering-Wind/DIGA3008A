It Takes Two Analysis

In an industry saturated with single-player narratives and online multiplayer experiences, It Takes Two (2021) stands out as a textbook example in cooperative gameplay and imaginative level design. Developed by Hazelight Studios, the game is built entirely around the concept of two players working in tandem, both narratively and mechanically. Rather than treating co-op as an optional mode, It Takes Two integrates collaboration as a core design philosophy, creating gameplay moments that are impossible to overcome alone. As Jose P. Zagal (2010) mentions, “to better understand a game it is important to understanding its components, how they interact, and how they facilitate certain experiences in players” (Zagal, 2010, p.33). This essay explores how the game’s narrative and mechanics are tightly interwoven, emphasising the importance of collaboration and communication within relationships. This will be illustrated by focusing on Chapter 1 of the game (“The Shed”) and by using Zagal’s “Ludoliteracy” lens (Zagal, 2010).

It Takes Two is a co-operative adventure game centred around May and Cody, a couple on the brink of divorce. They magically transform into dolls by their daughter Rose’s wish. Chapter 1: “The Shed” serves as the game’s introduction to both its story and its core cooperative gameplay mechanics. This can be illustrated through the narrative context and themes.

The narrative context and themes are shown throughout gameplay moments. The story begins with May and Cody informing their daughter Rose of their impending divorce, which prompts Rose to wish for her parents to reconcile. Her tears then animate her handmade dolls of her parent, trapping their consciousness inside these figures. This journey is checkpointed by Dr. Hakim, who is the anthropomorphic Book of Love, who also appears as a guide and therapist, insisting that the couple must work together to return to their human form. This dynamic ensures that the co-op mechanics are narratively tied into the theme, emphasising the importance of collaboration and communication with relationships. This ensures that Chapter 1 sets the emotional tone, highlighting the couple’s bitterness and inability to communicate effectively. Their bickering is mirrored in the narrative and gameplay while they overcome obstacles that require cooperation. During gameplay, the dialogue between both characters includes so much bickering. This can be illustrated in the gameplay mechanics as well. For instance, when a player throws something at the other, the following dialogue is something along the lines of “Oh Cody, grow up”.

This moves us onto the gameplay mechanics. Gameplay in Chapter 1 is designed as a series of puzzles and platforming challenges that can only be solved through collaboration. Each player controls one character (between May and Cody), and progression is impossible without both players actively participating. Early challenges include repairing a broken vacuum and navigating challenging environments in the shed. These challenges force players to communicate and synchronise their actions, reflecting the couple’s need to rebuild trust and partnership. The mechanics often require asymmetric cooperation, where each character has unique abilities or tools that complement the other. Like the challenge where one person operates the hammer while the other uses the nails, emphasising interdependence. This narrative-mechanic integration is what creates the balance within the game.

The obstacles in Chapter 1 are not just physical but metaphorical representations of the couple’s relationship issues. For instance, the players have to repair a broken vacuum cleaner, but it’s still a direct callback to an earlier argument, turning their domestic disputes into literal gameplay challenges. This takes the level design and challenges reinforce the message that progress (both in the game and in their relationship) is only possible through teamwork and empathy. Failure to cooperate results in setbacks, sort of mirroring the consequences of poor communication in real relationships.

The above-mentioned core mechanics all include elements that intertwine with collaboration. This includes the synchronised actions such as the puzzles that require precise timing and communication, obstacles that require both players to perform unique complementary actions and tool-based abilities. This is a good point to bring up a point Zagal makes, “In addition to being able to pick out elements of a game’s design, it is important to understand how the interaction between these elements helps create a certain experience for the player”(Zagal, 2010, p.33). These mechanics create a dynamic balance of co-operation and communication.

The art direction features whimsical, oversized objects highlighting the characters’ dependence on each other to overcome obstacles. The soundscape combines playful music with voice acting to capture the couple’s bickering and gradual reconciliation, providing narrative context that motivates players to work together, mirroring their in-game cooperation. Together, the art and sound design create a cohesive sensory experience that constantly reminds players of their mutual reliance. The visual scale and interactive elements frame the players as small and vulnerable, while the sound design provides emotional and practical cues that require attention and teamwork.

This creates a system that emphasises collaboration. As stated by Zagal, “to understand a game, it is often important to understand its context with relation to other games as well as gaming conventions and mechanics that might be common across multiple genres” (Zagal, 2010, p.31). It Takes Two strongly follows the conventions of an action-adventure game with strong platforming elements while also following the sub-genre of cooperative puzzle-platforming. However, it diverges in the sense that it is purely co-op with varied gameplay within Levels.

In summary, Chapter 1 of It Takes Two effectively introduces the game’s core theme of collaboration through tightly integrated narrative and gameplay. The asymmetric mechanics and cooperative challenges reflect the emotional tension between Cody and May, turning their relationship struggles into engaging gameplay. Viewed through Zagal’s lens, the chapter demonstrates how game elements work together to create a meaningful and reflective co-op experience that sets the tone for the rest of the game.
