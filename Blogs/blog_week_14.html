Approaching Justice and Ethics in Artistic and Programming Practices

by Amina Moosa 26 May 2025

Approaching justice and ethics in artistic and programming practices, such as game design, involves integrating social, political, and ethical values into the design process.

One of the key ways I believe this could be achieved is by game designers intentionally including values. Game designers should explicitly include values like inclusion and privacy in their design instead of letting these values be embedded by chance (Flanagan et al., 2007). This would mean actively thinking about which values matter for the game and its players.

Another way of approaching justice and ethics in game design is by finding values through playtesting. Testing the game and getting player feedback can help game designers uncover what players care about (Flanagan et al., 2007). This step is important to make sure the game's values match what players and society find important. This can then connect to turning those values into game features. For example, gender equity could mean adding diverse characters or features that appeal to females as well (Flanagan et al., 2007, p. 5). This then means that designers can resolve conflict by adjusting the design to include both values.

However, game design does often involve conflicting values, such as the market demand for sexualised characters versus the desire for fair representation. Designers do need to then handle these conflicts ethically and carefully, like seeking middle ground or even making explicit trade-offs while maintaining transparency about those decisions (Flanagan et al., 2007, p. 6).

While on the topic of ethics as well. I do agree that ethical game design should view players as capable of making thoughtful choices, and in doing so, games should include clear moral systems where actions have fitting rewards or consequences, letting players face real ethical challenges (Walk, 2018).

In addition, there should be an awareness of cultural and social contexts. Designers should then consider how their values and the game's values interact with the cultural norms and ethical beliefs of their player base. It would include being aware of hidden messages in the design system that might unintentionally support certain worldviews or social norms (Jerrett and Howell, 2022).

Ethical game design would also then mean avoiding biases and not letting one culture's values dominate others. So, in essence, designers should aim to include and respect different perspectives, especially in games with social or educational goals.

References:

  • Flanagan, M. and Nissenbaum, H. (2007) ‘A game design methodology to incorporate social activist themes’, in Proceedings of the SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems. CHI07: CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems, San Jose California USA: ACM, pp. 181–190.
  • Jerrett, A. and Howell, P.M. (2022). Values throughout the Game Space. Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction, 6(CHI PLAY), pp.1–27. doi: https://doi.org/10.1145/3549520
  • Walk, W. (2018). Ethics as a Game Mechanism. transcript Verlag eBooks, pp.193–216. doi: https://doi.org/10.1515/9783839443040-010.
