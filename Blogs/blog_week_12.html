Digital-Coloniality

by Amina Moosa 12 May 2025

How code matters in the context of decolonialism and just things one should be aware of in coding practices:

Programming languages are mostly shaped by the ideas and values of the West. This might result in an ignore of other cultures' ways of thinking, learning, and solving problems (since most coding is kinda done in English).

However, decolonial approaches suggest we should advocate for localisation and ethnocomputing (ethnocomputing being recognising and including traditional and indigenous ways of thinking about technology), even translanguaging (allowing people to use multiple languages together in learning and coding). (Geyser, 2024, pp. 12-16)

So this means that coding practices and open-source communities should prioritise the voices and participation of historically marginalised groups, including Indigenous peoples, Black communities, and others underrepresented in technology. This would involve not only inviting participation but also honouring diverse cultural practices, languages, and knowledge systems within the design and governance of technology (Arxiv.org, 2025)

And when doing research, I kind of gathered that decolonial coding would then involve equipping both educators and learners with the tools to critically interrogate code-reading, tracing, and writing it with an awareness of the power structures and epistemologies it may encode. (Geyser, 2024)

Thus, code literacies can help identify moments where colonial logic is embedded in programming concepts, tools, or practices. (Ali, 2016)

As a result, ethical and communal approaches include decolonial frameworks that emphasise communalism, universal good, and centring all communities in the development and deployment of technology, resulting in the benefit rather than exploitation of marginalised communities. (Arxiv.org, 2025)

Development Process Update

I am in the process of trying to incorporate JS into my navigation. In addition, I am also trying to navigate where I could add JS but also not abuse it. A lot of my current ideas are to make it look pretty and just spam JS everywhere, but this is kinda bad because it will definitely have a lag when looking at my website. My current plan of action includes drawing out ideas for where JS can be included in my website.

This helps streamline my interaction design by helping me consider how the website will behave and not just how it'll look. Since one of the key aspects of interaction design is centred on user behaviour, so I now have to consider how the product responds to user actions like clicks, taps, swipes, or form submissions and how I'll incorporate JS into such aspects.
