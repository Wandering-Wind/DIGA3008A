Essay 2: The Internet, Geopolitics, and Digital Coloniality

The internet, largely considered a space that does not have physical boundaries, is nonetheless shaped by distinct geographies that can often mirror or even reinforce global inequalities in power, representation, and access(Graham, 2014). From this arises digital coloniality, which describes how Western tech corporations show colonial patterns of control and extraction into the digital world, thus shaping infrastructure, data flows, and knowledge production to their advantage (Birhane, 2020). Both Graham (2014) and Birhane (2020) draw attention to the politics between the internet and digital colonialism and how they are closely intertwined. They do this by exploring how the technologies and algorithms behind our digital interactions often reinforce those patterns of inequality, just now in the digital form of the internet. A clear example of the above stated will be studied in this essay is the undersea cable outages in West Africa in March 2024, which exposed how digital infrastructure is still deeply tied to geopolitical power dynamics and colonial history, thus perpetuating systemic inequalities. This will be supported by the following frameworks: Abebe Birhane in Algorithmic Colonization of Africa (2020), Mark Graham in Internet Geographies (2014), and Christopher Lutz in Digital Inequalities in the Age of Artificial Intelligence and Big Data (2019).

On 14 March 2024, a suspected underwater rockslide off Côte d’Ivoire broke four major submarine cables. The impact was massive, resulting in a “near-total internet outage” that hit 13 countries, including Côte d’Ivoire, Benin, Liberia, and Ghana, while other countries like Nigeria and South Africa experienced partial disruptions (Internet Society, 2024). This undersea cable blackout exposed the vulnerabilities in Africa’s digital and internet infrastructure and thus showed how both environmental events and geopolitical realities can bring entire regions offline, particularly those that are heavily reliant on external systems. While regions like Europe managed to bounce back quickly, Africa’s repairs were delayed, mainly because the necessary ships were European-based (Munga, 2025).

As a result, key vulnerabilities were highlighted, such as the fact that the cable routes mirrored historical trade paths created during the colonial era (Mwema, 2024). Another key highlight worth mentioning is the infrastructure centralization, meaning that most of Africa’s internet traffic depends on undersea cables that are owned and controlled by Western tech giants such as Meta and Google (Mwema et al., 2024). This goes to show that infrastructure today still follows historical colonial footprints. Birhane (2020) uses the term algorithmic colonialism, basically describing how Africa is both separated from control and deeply dependent on global tech firms (Birhane, 2020, p. 391). Here is where the mirroring happens; it mirrors a colonial relationship where resources and value flow out, yet risks and instability remain on African nations. Consequently, the outages expose how little control African nations have over their connectivity, therefore reinforcing a pattern of technological dependency and exploitation.

On the topic of connectivity, when the blackout happened, it didn’t affect everyone equally. In some cases, they amplified existing digital inequalities, especially for already vulnerable or under-resourced countries. Mobile money platforms stalled, and people couldn’t transfer or access money. As a consequence of this outage, businesses lost revenue due to online sales, operations, or communications (Yusuf, 2024). Impacts such as these are classed as part of Lutz's (2019) digital inequalities framework, in which marginalized groups (already lacking continuous connectivity) bear the brunt of these disruptions (Lutz, 2019, p. 143). In essence, these disruptions hit marginalized people and economies where those that had few digital tools and less infrastructure suffered more. For instance, Ghana versus Europe is a prime example of the Lutz’s Third-Level Digital Divide (Lutz, 2019, p. 144). In this case, Ghana took five weeks to fully repair its internet cables, in contrast to European countries that recovered much faster from similar issues due to better infrastructure, funding, and control (Internet Society, 2024). This contrast illustrates Lutz’s Third-Level Digital Divide, meaning it is not just about who has internet or who knows how to use it, but also who can respond, adapt, and recover when something has gone wrong. This reflects the deeper structural inequalities in digital inequality by illustrating that it is not just a tech failure but a systemic imbalance in who controls, accesses, and benefits from these digital infrastructures.

Post-outage, many called for investment in redundancy, in essence, the building of better systems to avoid future blackouts (Yusuf, 2024). However, with the proposed solutions of backup cables or infrastructure upgrades, most of them are owned and controlled by Western tech companies (like Meta or Google). This means that while they’re framed as helping Africa, they actually deepen dependence and reinforce old colonial power dynamics. This aligns with Mwema Mozilla’s concept of digital colonialism, whereby big tech firms build infrastructure in the Global South, present it as generous or empowering, but as a consequence, retain control, extract data and profits, and leave local communities with little ownership (Mwema, 2024). Thus, bringing back the point of mirroring colonial systems whereby foreign powers build things to extract resources, not empower locals. This suggests that corporate-owned solutions may improve technical performance, but they don’t address a core issue: Africa still lacks sovereign control over its digital future. Hence, the term “digital colonialism”, an updated version of old power structures, just using cables this time.

In conclusion, the 2024 West African underwater cable outages showed us how the internet, generally regarded as being physically borderless, is highly subject to distinctive geogaphies that express geopolitical imbalances in power, representation, and access (Graham, 2014). Birhane (2020) and Graham (2014) both argue that Africa's digital infrastructure will continue to be reflective of colonial patterns of control as long as Africa's connectivity continues to heavily depend on Western-owned infrastructures. As a result, African nations are left with little control but high vulnerability, a dynamic described as algorithmic colonialism. This outage revealed digital inequalities. The delayed recovery in nations like Ghana was a reflection of the Third-Level Digital Divide, and as a result, marginalized communities suffered the most (Lutz, 2019). Instead of addressing dependency, post-outage solutions focused on infrastructure investments managed by Western companies. Without a move to regional data sovereignty, Africa's technology future will still be shaped by digital colonialism, although with new connections.
